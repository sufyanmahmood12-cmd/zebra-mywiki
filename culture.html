Popular Culture

In cultures outside of Africa, the zebra has long been thought of as a more exotic alternative to the horse. Comic book character Sheena, Queen of the Jungle is famously depicted riding a zebra, and explorer Osa Johnson was photographed riding one as well. Zebra stripe patterns have also become widely popular in fashion, furniture, body painting and architecture around the world.

Zebras in Art

In modern Western art, zebras have been popular subjects and inspirations for abstract, modernist and surrealist artists. Notable works include Christopher Wood's Zebra and Parachute(1930), Lucian Freud's The Painter's Room(1944) along with Quince on a Blue Table(1943 and 1944) with various paintings by Mary Fedden and Sidney Nolan. Victor Vasarely depicted zebras as interlocking black and white lines in a jigsaw puzzle fashion, becoming one of the most iconic artistic interpretations of the animal to date.

Carel Weight's Escape of the Zebra from the Zoo during an Air Raid was based on a real incident during the bombing of London Zoo in World War II, depicted across four comic book-style panels. Zebras have also lent themselves to commercial art and advertising, including for Zebra Grate Polish by British manufacturer Reckitt and Sons, and Japanese pen manufacturer Zebra Co., Ltd.

Film and Television

Zebras have become popular characters in animated film and television. They feature prominently in the Madagascar film franchise, The Lion King, and the animated film Khumba, as well as the children's television series Zou. These appearances have helped cement the zebra's status as one of the most beloved animals in popular entertainment.

The 2005 live-action film Racing Stripes features a captive zebra who is ostracised from horses on a farm and ends up being ridden by a rebellious girl, drawing on the long-standing idea of the zebra as an untameable, independent spirit.

Fashion and Design

The bold contrast of zebra stripes has made them a recurring motif in fashion and interior design. Zebra print has appeared on clothing, shoes, furniture, rugs and wallpaper across decades of design history. By the early 20th century, zebra skins were being used to make rugs and chairs, and the pattern itself has since been replicated synthetically across countless products worldwide.

Zebra stripe patterns are also used in body painting and have inspired architectural features and public art installations. The pattern's stark simplicity — pure black and white with no in-between — has given it a lasting visual power that designers continue to draw on today.

Proverbs and Language

The zebra holds a significant place in African language and oral tradition. The Maasai proverb "a man without culture is like a zebra without stripes" has become widely known across Africa, reflecting the deep symbolic importance of the animal's distinctive markings.

In the Shona language of Zimbabwe, the name madhuve means "woman/women of the zebra totem" and is used as a name for girls. For people of the African diaspora, the zebra has represented the politics of race and identity — being simultaneously black and white, belonging fully to neither and both at the same time.

In children's alphabet books worldwide, the zebra is almost universally used to represent the letter "Z" — a small but telling sign of just how embedded the animal has become in global culture.
