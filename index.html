Zebras (subgenus Hippotigris) are African equines with distinctive black-and-white striped coats. There are three living species of zebra: Grévy's zebra (Equus grevyi), the plains zebra (Equus quagga), and the mountain zebra (E. zebra). Zebra stripes come in different patterns, unique to each individual. Several theories have been proposed for the function of these patterns, with most evidence supporting them as a deterrent for biting flies. Zebras inhabit Eastern and Southern Africa and can be found in a variety of habitats such as savannas, grasslands, woodlands, and mountainous areas.

Etymology and Nomenclature

The English name "zebra" dates back to around the 1600s and was introduced via Italian, Spanish, or Portuguese explorers. The word traceably originated from the Old Galician-Portuguese term enzebro or ezebro, which was used in the Middle Ages to describe an aggressive wild donkey that once inhabited the Iberian Peninsula, and so, when Portuguese navigators traveled around the African continent and encountered striped equines, they applied this familiar name to them. In classical antiquity, Romans referred to the animal as hippotigris (which translates to "horse-tiger"), a term that is still utilized in modern biological classification.

Taxonomy and Evolution

Zebras belong to the Equus genus, sharing a smiliar lineage to horses and donkeys. They diverged from other equines within the last 4 million years. While historically classified into types of subgenera like categorizing the plains and mountainous zebras under Hippotigris and the Grévy's zebra under Dolichohippus, more recent taxonomic revisions actually place all three living species under the single subgenus of Hippotigris .

Fossil record

In addition to the three living species, some fossil zebras and relatives have also been identified. E. oldowayensis is identified from remains in Olduvai Gorge dating to 1.8 mya (1,8 million). Fossil skulls of E. mauritanicus from Algeria which date to around 1 mya (1 million years) appearing to show affinities with the plains zebra and E. capensis, also known as the Cape zebra, which appeared around 2 mya (2 million years) and lived throughout Southern and Eastern Africa.

Hybrids

In terms of hybrids, fertile hybrids have been reported in the wild between plains and Grévy's zebra. Hybridisation has also been recorded between the plains and mountain zebra, though it is possible that these are infertile due to the difference in chromosome numbers between the two species. Captive zebras have been bred with horses and donkeys, the offspring of which are referred to as zebroids. A zorse is a cross between a zebra and a horse, a zonkey, between a zebra and a donkey and a zony, between a zebra and a pony. Zebroids are often born sterile with dwarfism.

Characteristics

As with all wild equines, zebras have barrel-chested bodies with tufted tails, elongated faces and long necks with long, erect manes. Their thin legs are each supported by a spade-shaped toe covered in a hard hoof. Their dentition is adapted for grazing, they have large incisors that clip grass blades and rough molars and premolars well suited for grinding. Males have spade-shaped canines, which can be used as weapons in fighting. The eyes of zebras are at the sides and far up the head, which allows them to look over the tall grass while feeding. Their moderately long, pointed ears are movable and are able to locate the source of a sound.

Stripes

The most recognizable characteristic of a Zebra is their bold black and white stripes. Despite appearances, their skin is actually black while the coat is white with black stripes layered on top. Foals are born with brown and white coats that darken with age. The purpose of zebra stripes has been debated by biologists for over a century with several theories existing, including camouflage, to confuse predators, temperature regulation and social recognition. However, the most strongly supported explanation believed today is its protective qualities against flies. Studies have shown that the black and white pattern deters blood sucking flies like horse flies and tsetse flies from landing, which is significant as these insects carry diseases lethal to equines. Interestingly, horses dressed in zebra-print coats show the same fly-repelling effect, suggesting the pattern itself is what actually matters.

Social Structure, Ecology and Behavior

Zebras may travel or migrate to wetter areas during dry seasons. Plains zebras have been recorded traveling up to 500km between Namibia and Botswana,which has been recorded as the longest land migration of mammals in Africa to date. When they do migrate, they appear to rely on memory of the locations where foraging conditions are the best. Plains zebras are more water dependant, so they live in moister environments than other species. Grévy's zebras can survive up to a week without water but will drink it every day when given the chance, as their bodies maintain water better than cattle. Mountain zebras require an abundant and regular supply of drinking water due to their unique biology, needing to drink at least once a day under normal conditions.

A zebra's diet consists of mostly grass and sedges, but they will, if they have the opportunity or need, consume bark, leaves, buds, fruits, and roots. Compared to ruminants, zebras have a simpler and less efficient digestive system. Nevertheless, they can subsist on lower-quality vegetation. Zebras may spend up to 60–80% of their time feeding, depending on the availability of vegetation. The plains zebra is a pioneer grazer, mowing down the upper, less nutritious grass canopy and preparing the way for more specialized grazers like wildebeest, which depend on shorter and more nutritious grasses.

Zebras are prey mainly to lions, leopards, cheetahs, spotted hyenas, brown hyenas and wild dogs, who pose less of a threat to adults. Biting and kicking are a zebra's main defense tactics against predators. When threatened by lions, zebras flee, and when caught, they are rarely effective in fighting them off. A strategy utilized by zebras while being attacked is to huddle up in groups, if possible, and kick. In one study, the maximum speed of a zebra was found to be 50 km/h (or 31 mph) while a lion was measured at 74 km/h (46 mph), which suggests zebras are not able to escape lions through speed alone but by sideways turning, especially when the lion is close behind. With smaller predators, however, such as hyenas and dogs, zebras may act more aggressively, especially in defense of their young, attempting to fight and ward off the threat.

Social Behavior

Zebra species have two basic social structures. Plains and mountain zebras live in stable, closed family groups or harems consisting of one stallion, several mares, and their offspring. These groups have their own home ranges, which overlap, and they tend to be nomadic. Stallions form and expand their harems by herding young mares away from their birth harems. The stability of the group remains even when the family stallion is displaced. Females in harems can spend more time feeding, and gain protection both from stallions and from their young. They have a linear dominance hierarchy with the high-ranking females having lived in the group longest. While traveling, the most dominant females and their offspring lead the group, followed by the next most dominant, who is the family stallion, trailing behind. Young of both sexes leave their natal groups as they mature with females usually herded by outside males to become part of their harems.

The more arid-living Grévy's zebras have a more open social hierarchy, where adults have more fluid associations with other adult males, establishing large territories marked by dung piles, mating with the females that enter them. Grazing and drinking areas tend to be separated in these environments and the most dominant males establish territories near watering holes, which attract females with dependent foals and those who simply want a drink, while less dominant males control territories away from water with more vegetation, and only attract mares without foals.

In all species, excess males gather in bachelor groups. These are typically young males that are not yet ready to establish a harem or territory. With the plains zebra, the oldest males are the most dominant and group membership is stable. Bachelor groups tend to be at the boundaries of herds and during group movements, the bachelors follow behind or along the sides. Mountain zebra bachelor groups may also include young females that have left their natal group early, as well as old, former harem males. A territorial Grévy's zebra stallion may allow non-territorial bachelors in their territory, however, when a mare is in the phase of being sexually receptive and ready to mate, also known as oestrus , the territorial stallion keeps other stallions at bay. Bachelors prepare for their future harem roles with play fights and greeting/challenge rituals, which make up most of their day to day activities.

Grooming

Plains and mountain zebras strengthen their social bonds through grooming. Members of a harem nibble and rake along each other's neck, shoulder and back with their teeth and lips. Grooming typically occurs between mothers and foals as well as between stallions and mares, helping to establish social rank and ease aggressive behaviour. Grévy's zebras, however, generally do not perform social grooming, due to a lack of permanent social bonds and fluid, temporary groups.

Reproduction

Plains and mountain zebra females mate only with their harem stallion, while Grévy's zebras are more promiscuous with who they mate with. Female zebras have oestrous cycles of five to ten days, with the period of time between conception and birth, also known as gestation lasts around a year. Newborn zebras will follow anything that moves, so mothers keep other mares away until the foal learns to recognize her by stripe pattern, smell and voice. Foals begin grazing within weeks but may continue nursing for up to thirteen months.

When threatened by predators, plains and mountain zebra groups form a protective front with foals in the centre while the stallion charges at attackers. Grévy's zebra foals stay in group "kindergartens" tended by the territorial male when mothers leave for water. Notably, plains zebra stallions are intolerant of foals that aren't theirs and may practice infanticide, killing them.

Sleeping

Zebras sleep up to seven hours a day. During the day they doze standing up by locking their knee joints, allowing them to bolt quickly if a predator approaches. For deeper sleep, they lie down on their sides at night, usually only doing so when other members of the group are awake and keeping watch.

Fights

Fights between males usually occur over mates and involve biting and kicking. In plains zebra herds, stallions fight each other over recently matured mares to bring into their group, while the mare's father will fight off all suitors trying to abduct her. As long as a harem stallion is healthy, he is not usually challenged. Only unhealthy stallions have their harems taken over, and even then, the new stallion slowly takes over, peacefully displacing the old one. Agonistic behavior between male Grévy's zebras usually occurs at the border of their territories.

Communication

Zebras produce a number of vocalizations and noises in order to talk to each other. The plains zebra has a distinctive, barking contact call heard as "a-ha, a-ha, a-ha" or "kwa-ha, kaw-ha, ha, ha". Loud snorting and rough "gasping" in zebras signals alarm. Squealing is usually made when in pain, but can also be heard in friendly interactions. Zebras also communicate with visual displays, with the flexibility of their lips allowing them to make complex facial expressions. Visual displays also consist of head, ear, and tail postures and movements. A zebra may signal an intention to kick by dropping back its ears and whipping its tail. Flattened ears, bared teeth and a waving head may be used as threatening gestures by stallions.

Cultural Significance

With their distinctive black and white stripes, zebras are among the most recognizable animals in the world. They have been associated with beauty and grace, with naturalist Thomas Pennant, in 1781, describing them as "the most elegant of quadrupeds". Zebras have a substantial popularity in photography as well, with some wildlife photographers describing them as the most photogenic animal. They have become staples in children's stories and wildlife-themed art, such as depictions of Noah's Ark and the widely recognized Zebra(1937) by Victor Vasarely.

Zebras have been featured in African art and culture for millennia. They have been depicted in rock art across Southern Africa, which dates anyhwere between 20 to 28 thousand years ago, though less often than antelope species like eland. How the zebra got its stripes has also been the subject of folk tales, some of which involve it being scorched by fire. The San people connected zebra stripes with water, rain and lightning, and water spirits were conceived of having these markings.

For the Shona people, the zebra is a totem animal and is glorified in a poem titled "Zebra", from Zimbabwe, as an "iridescent and glittering creature". Its stripes have symbolized the union of males and females. At the ruined city of Great Zimbabwe, zebra stripes decorate what is believed to be a domba, a school meant to prepare girls for adulthood. In the Shona language, the name "madhuve" means "woman/women of the zebra totem" and is a name for girls in Zimbabwe. The plains zebra is the national animal of Botswana and zebras have been depicted on stamps during colonial and post-colonial Africa. For people of the African diaspora, the zebra represented the politics of race and identity, being both black and white.

Conservation Efforts

As of 2016 to 2019, the IUCN Red List of mammals lists Grévy's zebra as endangered, the mountain zebra as vulnerable and the plains zebra as near-threatened. Grévy's zebra populations are estimated at less than 2,000 mature individuals, but remain stable. Mountain zebras number near 35,000 individuals and their population appears to be increasing. Plains zebra are estimated to number 150,000–250,000 with a decreasing population trend. Human intervention has fragmented zebra ranges and populations as their population numberd are threatened by hunting for their hide and meat alongside habitat destruction. They also compete with livestock and have their traveling routes obstruct by fences. Civil wars in some countries have also caused declines in zebra populations. By the early 20th century, zebra skins were being used to make rugs and chairs. In the 21st century, zebras may be taken by trophy hunters as zebra skin rugs sell for $1,000 to $2,000. Trophy hunting was rare among African peoples though the San were known to hunt zebra for meat.

However, measures have taken place to ensure the species survive. Zebras can be found in numerous protected areas. Important areas for Grévy's zebra include Yabelo Wildlife Sanctuary and Chelbi Sanctuary in Ethiopia and Buffalo Springs, Samburu and Shaba National Reserves in Kenya. The plains zebra inhabits the Serengeti National Park in Tanzania, Tsavo and Masai Mara in Kenya, Hwange National Park in Zimbabwe, Etosha National Park in Namibia, and Kruger National Park in South Africa. Mountain zebras are protected in Mountain Zebra National Park, Karoo National Park and Goegap Nature Reserve in South Africa as well as Etosha and Namib-Naukluft Park in Namibia.
