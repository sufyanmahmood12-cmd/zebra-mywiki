History in Captivity

Zebras have been kept in captivity since at least the age of the Roman Empire. In later times, captive zebras were shipped around the world, often for diplomatic purposes. These animals were prized as exotic gifts between rulers and heads of state.

In 1261, Sultan Baibars of Egypt established an embassy with Alfonso X of Castile and sent a zebra along with other exotic animals as gifts. In 1417, a zebra was gifted to the Chinese people by Somalia and displayed before the Yongle Emperor Zhu Di .

The fourth Mughal emperor Jahangir received a zebra from Ethiopia in 1620 with the court painter Ustad Mansur making a detailed painting of it, now knows as one of the earliest known artistic depictions of a zebra in Asia. In the 1670s, Ethiopian Emperor Yohannes I exported two zebras to the Dutch governor of Jakarta. These animals were eventually gifted by the Dutch to the Tokugawa Shogunate of Japan.

In 1882, Ethiopia sent a zebra to French president Jules Grévy. The species it belonged to was subsequently named Equus grevyi in his honor, thus giving us the name Grévy's zebra.

Queen Charlotte's Zebra

When Queen Charlotte received a zebra as a wedding gift in 1762, the animal became a source of fascination for the people of Britain. Many flocked to see it at its paddock in Buckingham Palace.

The zebra soon became the subject of humour and satire, being referred to by the public as "The Queen's Ass". It was also the subject of an oil painting by the renowned artist George Stubbs in 1763. The animal gained a reputation for being ill-tempered and frequently kicked at visitors who came to view it.

Domestication Attempts

Attempts to domesticate zebras have been largely unsuccessful. It is thought that having evolved under pressure from the many large predators of Africa — including early humans — zebras became more aggressive and unpredictable than horses, making domestication far more difficult.

However, zebras have been trained by man in some cases throughout history. In Rome, zebras are recorded to have pulled chariots during amphitheatre games starting in the reign of Emperor Caracalla (198 to 217 AD). This remains one of the earliest documented cases of zebras being used by humans for labour purposes.

In the late 19th century, the zoologist Walter Rothschild trained a small number of zebras to draw a carriage in England, which he famously drove to Buckingham Palace to demonstrate that zebras could be trained. However, he chose not to ride them, knowing that they were too small and aggressive to be ridden safely.

In the early 20th century, German colonial officers in East Africa attempted to use zebras for both driving and riding, with only limited success. These efforts further confirmed that while zebras could be partially trained, full domestication comparable to that of horses was not achievable.

Zebras in Zoos

Today, all three species of zebra can be found in zoos and wildlife parks across the world. Zebras are among the most popular zoo animals due to their striking appearance and active social behaviour. They are frequently kept in mixed-species enclosures alongside other African animals to replicate their natural savannah environment.

Captive zebra populations also play an important role in conservation efforts. Studbook programmes coordinate breeding between institutions to maintain genetic diversity, particularly for the Grévy's zebra, which is listed as endangered with fewer than 2,000 mature individuals remaining in the wild.
