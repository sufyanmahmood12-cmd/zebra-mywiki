Fun Fact Number 1!

Each zebra's stripe pattern is completely unique — no two are alike, just like human fingerprints. Scientists can actually identify individual zebras in the wild just by their stripes.

Fun Fact Number 2!

Like their horse relatives, zebras can sleep while standing by locking their knee joints. This adaptation allows them to wake up quickly and flee from predators. However, for deeper sleep, zebras often lie down on their sides.

Fun Fact Number 3!

Whilst you wouldn't consider black and white stripes as good camouflage it works well for a zebra. Their main predators are lions which are colour blind so if the zebras bunch together it confuses a lion as they could think their stripes are grass.

Fun Fact Number 4!

Zebras have amazing eyesight and are some of the few mammals that can actually see colour. The only colour they can't see is orange.

Fun Fact Number 5!

Zebras have amazing hearing and their ears allow them to hear in almost any direction. They are also able to communicate their mood to other zebras with their ears.

Fun Fact Number 6!

Zebras possess exceptional night vision, which gives them a significant advantage over many predators. Their large eyes and well-developed night vision help them spot danger in low-light conditions, making dawn and dusk safaris particularly exciting times to observe their natural behaviors.

Fun Fact Number 7!

Zebras are ecosystem engineers. As they graze, they keep grasslands healthy by preventing any single plant species from dominating. Their grazing patterns create a mosaic of different grass heights, which benefits other animals.

Fun Fact Number 8!

Within just one hour of being born, a baby zebra can stand and walk, and within a few hours, it can keep up with the herd. This rapid development is essential for survival in the wild, where staying with the protective group is crucial for avoiding predators.

Fun Fact Number 9!

A baby zebra identifies its mother by her stripes, which have a unique pattern.

Fun Fact Number 10!

A zebra's teeth grow continuously throughout its lifetime, offsetting the wear of constant grazing.
